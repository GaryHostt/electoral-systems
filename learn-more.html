üåç Electoral Systems Around the World

Here's where these systems are actually used in real elections:

System Name Type of Race/Location Specific Examples
1First-Past-the-Post (FPTP) Single-winner majoritarian for legislative and executive bodies United Kingdom, United States (House seats), Canada, India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Belarus
2Two-Round System (TRS) Single-winner majoritarian, most commonly for presidential elections France, Finland, Austria, Portugal, Poland, Russia, many Latin American countries, Francophone Africa (Mali, Ivory Coast)
3Instant-Runoff Voting (IRV/RCV) Single-winner ranked voting system Australian House of Representatives, Papua New Guinea Parliament, Irish Presidential election. US Cities: San Francisco, Portland ME, St. Paul MN
4Party-List PR (Closed List) Multi-winner proportional representation Israel (nationwide list), Spain, Portugal, Russia, Kazakhstan, South Africa
5Party-List PR (Open List) Multi-winner proportional representation with candidate preference Finland, Latvia, Sweden, Netherlands, Brazil
6Single Transferable Vote (STV) Multi-winner proportional representation Republic of Ireland (parliament and presidential), Malta, Australian Senate. Regional: Northern Ireland, Scotland (local elections)
7Mixed-Member Proportional (MMP) Mixed system with compensatory list seats for overall proportionality Germany (inventor), New Zealand, Scottish Parliament (AMS), Welsh Assemblies (AMS)
8Parallel Voting (MMM) Mixed system where district and list seats are calculated independently Japan, Russia, Taiwan, Lithuania, Thailand
9Block Voting Multi-winner, winner-take-all plurality in multi-seat constituencies Philippines Senate (at-large), Laos National Assembly, Marshall Islands Legislature, Palau Senate
10Limited Voting Multi-winner, semi-proportional where voters have fewer votes than seats Spain (Senate), Argentina (Chamber of Deputies, formerly), UK (historically)
11Approval Voting Single-winner system where voters approve any number of candidates US Cities: St. Louis MO, Fargo ND (formerly). Used by many academic and professional societies
